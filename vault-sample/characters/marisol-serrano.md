# Marisol Serrano

The body in the grove — and the person the book refuses to let stay a body.
Marisol was an adult woman living at the county's margins: seasonal packing
work when the sheds were hiring, cleaning shifts when they weren't, a kid she
was raising mostly alone, a sister she did laundry with on Tuesdays. The
apparatus filed her under *nobody* while she was alive and under *victim
profile* once she wasn't. The novel's job is to reverse both filings.

## Craft notes (how to write a victim without embalming her)

- She appears in three registers, and they must never blur:
  1. **The case** — photographs, measurements, the ME's language. Cold on
     purpose; this is the county's version.
  2. **Lucia's memory** — laundromat heat, a shared cigarette, an argument
     about the kid's school. Warm, specific, unsentimental.
  3. **Vega's reconstruction** — the register that convicts him, because he
     is good enough to imagine her whole and does it thirty years too late.
- Give her one skill the story doesn't need (she could name every bird in
  the river wash). Uselessly human details are the point.

## The abduction (canon)

Taken via a ruse — a car she half-recognized, a voice saying there was a
ride, "**I got a ride, sis**" called back through the laundromat door while
Lucia fed quarters to the dryer. Ten days later she was found in the dying
grove off S16. What happened in between is the forensic spine of the case
(see the grove and institution pins on the map): held alive for days in a
controlled institutional interior, ligature-strangled, washed, kept cool,
staged among the dead trees to read as a backcountry passion killing.

## Open questions

- OPEN: how much of Marisol's own voice to allow — current lean is one
  scene, very early, before the reader knows who she is.
- OPEN: what she knew about the Ring, and whether she was taken because
  she was convenient or because she was *specifically* dangerous to someone.
