# Judge Gerald Crane

The antagonist is not a man but a machine; Crane is the machine's most
presentable face. In 1994 he was the assistant district attorney who decided
which cases got oxygen — and the Serrano case, with its inconvenient
gravity toward powerful men, got none. In 2024 he is a senior judge, a
courthouse namesake in waiting, and the quiet convener of the hunt for Lucia
that wears the costume of a cold-case review.

## Voice

- Courteous, unhurried, genuinely charming. Crane has never raised his voice
  because he has never needed to; the system raises it for him. Write him
  with zero mustache-twirl — his menace is administrative. The scariest
  things he says should be scheduling decisions.
- He speaks of 1994 with fond, wistful inaccuracy — nostalgia as a
  filing system for guilt.

## Function

- He is the **heir and executor** of the Ring rather than merely a member:
  some of the men Lucia took were his patrons, and their surviving families
  are his constituency. The 2024 hunt is estate management.
- Crane vs. Vega is the theme stated as a duel: Crane knows the difference
  between truth and justice better than anyone in the book — and has made
  the difference his career. Vega pays for the difference; Crane collects
  on it.

## Contradictions

- Believes, sincerely, that he kept the peace — that a county fed a story
  is a county spared a fire. His self-image is custodial, almost tender.
- Meticulous about the law's letter in exact proportion to his contempt for
  its object.

## Key scenes

- Ch. 3: first 2024 appearance — a courtesy visit to the interrogation
  suite, ninety seconds, one question, and the room's true ownership is
  established.
- Ch. 5: the 1994 flashback where a younger Crane closes the Serrano file
  with a phrase Vega will spend thirty years unable to forget ("no
  prosecutorial merit").
- Ch. 8: Crane wins — in public. The dark ending's ballast. Whatever the
  sliver of light is, it does not touch him yet. Cardenas's memory is aimed
  at him like a slow instrument.
