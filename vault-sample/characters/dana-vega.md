# Dana Vega

The marriage plot is not a subplot; it is the flaw demonstrated at home. Dana
has two affairs. Vega — the great detective — finds **Lover #1**, files the
grievance, and stops looking, satisfied. He never suspects **Lover #2 is
Brennan**. The reader gets the same evidence Vega gets and should be able to
solve Dana before he does. That's the game: the book trains you to out-detect
the detective in his own house, so you'll trust yourself when the case
inverts.

## Voice

- In 1994 scenes she is always mid-motion — leaving rooms, keys in hand.
  Vega's narration renders her in fragments because he attends to her in
  fragments; the prose enacts the neglect.
- Give Dana two or three scenes of *her own competence* (she manages the
  household's collapse with more rigor than Vega manages the case) so the
  reader understands the affairs are not a mystery. Loneliness with a
  paper trail.

## Contradictions

- She married Vega for the quality that ends the marriage: total attention,
  which turned out to be aimable at exactly one thing at a time, and never
  at her.
- Lover #1 is almost a decoy she lets him find — easier to be caught at the
  lesser thing. Whether that is strategy or exhaustion, keep ambiguous.

## Secrets

1. Brennan. Tender, unhurried, and — this is the knife — built on the same
   thing Vega loved in him: steadiness, belief, presence. Hidden edge;
   revealed ch. 8, after both men are past being confronted.
2. She knew Vega was drowning in the Serrano case in the spring of 1994 and
   knew, with a wife's precision, the week he started lying about it.
   She never learned what the lie was. OPEN: does she learn in 2024?

## Key scenes

- Ch. 2: Vega discovers Lover #1 — and the scene is played as *relief*, his
  and maybe hers. The reader should feel the wrongness of that relief.
- Ch. 6: the last dinner; two true sentences almost get said.
- Ch. 8 (or the 2024 frame): the double mirror named at last. Whether Dana
  is alive in 2024 is OPEN — current lean: alive, remarried, unafraid of
  him, the only person who was ever allowed to pity him.
